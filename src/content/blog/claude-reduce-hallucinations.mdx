**TL;DR**: Anthropic's own documentation includes three system prompt instructions that dramatically reduce hallucinations. They're not hidden — just buried. Here's what they are and when to use them.

---

I kept getting confident-sounding outputs from Claude with zero sources. The kind of stuff that sounds right but you can't verify.

Then I stumbled into Anthropic's "Reduce Hallucinations" documentation page. Found three system prompt instructions that changed everything.

---

## The Three Instructions

### 1. "Allow Claude to say I don't know"

Without this, Claude fills knowledge gaps with plausible fiction. With it, you actually get "I don't have enough information to answer that."

Sounds simple. But the default behavior is to always give an answer, even when it shouldn't.

**Add to your system prompt:**
```
If you don't have enough information to answer accurately, say "I don't know" or "I'm not certain." Do not guess or fill gaps with plausible-sounding information.
```

### 2. "Verify with citations"

Tell Claude every claim needs a source. If it can't find one, it should retract the claim.

I watched statements vanish from outputs when I turned this on. Statements that sounded authoritative before suddenly had no backing.

**Add to your system prompt:**
```
Every factual claim must include a citation. If you cannot provide a source for a claim, retract the claim rather than stating it without evidence.
```

### 3. "Use direct quotes for factual grounding"

Force Claude to extract word-for-word quotes from documents before analyzing them. This stops the paraphrase-drift where the model subtly changes meaning while summarizing.

**Add to your system prompt:**
```
When referencing documents or sources, extract direct quotes before providing analysis. Use the exact wording from the source material rather than paraphrasing.
```

---

## Each One Helps. All Three Together Transform Output Quality.

Individually, these instructions improve accuracy. Combined, they fundamentally change how Claude handles uncertainty and evidence.

**Before:** Confident statements that sound authoritative but can't be verified.

**After:** Hedged statements with sources, or honest admissions of uncertainty.

---

## The Tradeoff

There's a cost. A paper (arXiv 2307.02185) found that citation constraints reduce creative output.

These instructions make Claude more accurate but less generative. You don't want them on all the time.

**Solution: Build a toggle.**

- **Research mode:** All three instructions active. Use for fact-checking, research, analysis.
- **Default mode:** Instructions off. Use for brainstorming, creative writing, exploration.

Someone already built this as a CLI tool: [github.com/assafkip/research-mode](https://github.com/assafkip/research-mode)

---

## The Combined System Prompt

For research and fact-checking tasks, add all three to your system prompt:

```
ACCURACY INSTRUCTIONS:

1. If you don't have enough information to answer accurately, say "I don't know" or "I'm not certain." Do not guess or fill gaps with plausible-sounding information.

2. Every factual claim must include a citation. If you cannot provide a source for a claim, retract the claim rather than stating it without evidence.

3. When referencing documents or sources, extract direct quotes before providing analysis. Use the exact wording from the source material rather than paraphrasing.
```

---

## Why This Matters

The weird part: this is published on Anthropic's own platform docs. Not hidden. But most people building on Claude have never seen it.

**Source:** [docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/test-and-evaluate/strengthen-guardrails/reduce-hallucinations](https://docs.anthropic.com/en/docs/test-and-evaluate/strengthen-guardrails/reduce-hallucinations)

If you're using Claude for anything where accuracy matters — research, analysis, fact-checking, professional work — these three instructions should be in your toolkit.
